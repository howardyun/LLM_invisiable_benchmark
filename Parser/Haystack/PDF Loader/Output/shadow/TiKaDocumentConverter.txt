Abstract

Neural Radiance Fields (NeRF) and 3D Gaussian Splatting001
(3DGS) have demonstrated strong capability in reconstructing 3D002
scenes ranging from indoor to outdoor environments. For large-003
scale scenes, reconstruction is often accelerated through paral-004
lelization based on geographic partitioning. However, excessive005
scene size introduces viewpoint locality, leading to two major chal-006
lenges: reconstruction isolation and redundant computation. The007
former causes significant inconsistency in rendering quality across008
viewpoints and the latter leads to repetitive optimization of similar009
elements, resulting in wasted computational resources. Inspired010
by the expert optimization principle in Mixture-of-Experts (MoEs),011
we propose EGGS (Expert-Guided Framework Constructed from012
Semantic Priors for Large-Scale Scene Reconstruction), an expert-013
guided framework that replaces geographic partitioning with ex-014
pert collaboration constructed from semantic priors. EGGS as-015
signs experts to scene elements based on semantic and feature016
similarity, enabling semantic-aware collaboration that effectively017

alleviates isolation and redundancy. Extensive experiments on 018
both synthetic and real-world datasets demonstrate that EGGS 019
achieves state-of-the-art reconstruction quality, outperforming ex- 020
isting methods by up to 5% in PSNR. Code and datasets will be 021
released upon acceptance. 022

2. Related Work 101

2.1. Large-scale Scene Reconstruction 102

The conflict between high reconstruction costs and low accuracy 103
in large-scale scene reconstruction, driven by scene complexity, re- 104
mains a key challenge in 3D reconstruction. Recent NeRF variants 105
using heuristic partitioning [38, 39, 44] or voxel grids [2, 6, 26, 45] 106
have supported performance in large-scale scenes but are lim- 107
ited by long training times. Alternatively, 3D Gaussian Splatting 108
(3DGS) has introduced methods like progressive [14, 17, 49] and 109
level-of-detail (LOD) schemes [21, 30] to balance quality and ef- 110
ficiency. However, these geographically partitioned methods still 111
suffer from inconsistent reconstruction quality due to viewpoint 112
limitations, leading to degraded overall results. 113

2.2. Semantic-awareness 114

With advancements in 2D semantic understanding [15, 29], recent 115
research has explored semantic integration in 3D reconstruction 116
[3, 8, 28, 47]. Some methods embed semantic information into 117
explicit 3D Gaussian representations by projecting 2D semantic 118
features or aggregating semantic pools [7]. Others leverage ex- 119
ternal semantic models to guide 3DGS training or inference [51], 120
such as introducing semantic viewpoints consistency losses to mit- 121
igate overfitting [11, 41]. These efforts highlight the potential of 122
semantic-aware methods in 3D reconstruction [27], yet their appli- 123
cation in complex, large-scale scenes requires further exploration. 124
Inspired by the growing field of 3D spatial semantic awareness, 125
we aim to leverage rich semantic information in large-scale scenes 126
to overcome viewpoint limitations and enable optimization regard- 127
less of spatial distance. 128

2.3. MoEs and Multi-Expert Framework 129

MoEs framework [10, 12, 35]enables parallel training through ex- 130
pert specialization. Recent successes in visual imaging [31, 46] 131
have inspired its application in 3D reconstruction. Existing work 132
has combined MoEs with 3D reconstruction [4, 5, 19, 34] and 133
made improvements for large-scale scenes [24, 50]. However, 134
prior works have largely ignored the role of experts as reusable 135
semantic priors capable of guiding similar objects that frequently 136
appear in large-scale scenes. 137

3.1. Overview146

EGGS is a large-scale collaborative reconstruction framework147
based on a semantic partitioned multi-expert model. Instead148
of dividing scenes into geographic blocks, EGGS establishes a149
complete reconstruction pipeline through two major systems: an150
expert-guided framework and expert collaborative reconstruction.151
These correspond to three key modules: Semantic-Prior Filter-152
ing, Expert Division & Guidance and Expert Collaborative Re-153
construction.154

The semantic prior filtering module employs a dual filtering155
mechanism based on semantics and clustering to select viewpoints156
and corresponding point clouds that are semantically rich and ex-157
hibit strong multi-view consistency from large-scale scenes. These158
selected viewpoints are prioritized during reconstruction, produc-159
ing high-quality Gaussian anchors that form category-specific ex-160
perts representing different semantic classes.161

The expert-guided reconstruction module treats these experts162
as reconstruction priors. During the preliminary reconstruction163
stage, EGGS performs stable matching between experts and spa-164
tially scattered yet semantically consistent elements across the165
scene, enabling one-to-one expert guidance. Through a graph neu-166
ral network, prior knowledge is propagated from experts to guide167
the reconstruction of these elements, achieving high-quality and168
balanced results. This design effectively mitigates the reconstruc-169
tion quality disparity and redundant computation “rediscovered”170
and “relearned”) caused by viewpoint locality and isolated render-171
ing of repeated structures.172

Finally, the expert collaboration mechanism addresses the in-173
trinsic competition–cooperation problem introduced by replacing174
geographic partitioning with a multi-expert framework. By as-175
signing gradient weights based on rendering quality, EGGS pro-176
motes expert cooperation during reconstruction. Additionally, an177
opacity-based inter-expert constraint reduces optimization con-178

flicts at expert boundaries, alleviating edge blurring and ensuring 179
coherent global reconstruction. 180

3.2. Expert-Guided Framework 181

3.2.1. Semantic-Prior Filtering. 182

Semantic-prior filtering module of EGGS takes camera view- 183
points, point clouds, and semantic segmentation masks as input to 184
generate a subset of viewpoints and point clouds prioritized for ex- 185
pert reconstruction. This module incorporates a Segmented-Prior 186
filtering mechanism consisting of Segmented Filter and viewpoint 187
clustering. The semantic segmentation mask prior is obtained us- 188
ing the CLIP+SAM[15, 29] model guided by textual prompts to 189
capture semantic regions, as detailed in the supplementary mate- 190
rial, including model parameters and settings. Semantic score fil- 191
tering selects the viewpoints and point clouds that best represent 192
expert-specific reconstruction regions, while viewpoint clustering 193
ensures viewpoints consistency within each expert model. 194

3.2.2. Expert Division & Guidance.233

Expert Construction. The reconstruction isolation and redundant234
computation issues caused by viewpoint locality essentially stem235
from the absence of a unified global paradigm to guide and con-236
strain reconstruction across spatially distributed objects. Under237
limited viewpoint coverage, identical structures cannot be consis-238
tently captured from a global perspective, resulting in fragmented239
reconstructions.240

To address this, EGGS constructs multiple category-specific241
experts using the semantically rich viewpoints and correspond-242
ing point clouds obtained from the semantic prior filtering mod-243
ule. These selected data are first reconstructed through a priority244
reconstruction process, generating high-quality Gaussian anchors245
that serve as the foundation of each expert. Each category expert is246
composed of Gaussian anchors corresponding to its semantic cat-247
egory and acts as a global prior to guide consistent reconstruction248
across the entire scene, as illustrated in Fig. 2.249

Stage 1: Multi-Scale Stable Matching. The stable matching250
algorithm connects the multi-category experts obtained from pri-251
ority reconstruction with their corresponding semantic instances252
across the scene, regardless of geographic separation. By estab-253
lishing associations purely based on feature similarity rather than254
spatial proximity, this process eliminates reconstruction isolation255
caused by inconsistent reconstruction of semantically identical ob-256
jects distributed across large-scale scenes.257

Considering that the classical stable matching algorithm has258
a computational complexity of O(n2), performing global feature259
association among all Gaussian anchors is computationally pro-260
hibitive. To address this, we propose a multi-scale stable matching261
scheme based on an Octree hierarchical structure, which reduces262
the overall time complexity from O(n2) to O(n logn), thus meet-263
ing the efficiency requirements of expert-guided reconstruction in264
large-scale scenarios, as shown in Fig. 3.265

4.1. Experimental Setup 358

Dataset Configuration. EGGS is evaluated on five large-scale 359
datasets, including one synthetic urban dataset (MatrixCity[16]), 360
two real-world datasets (Hospital and P1 HuiTian), and two com- 361
plex scenes (Residence and ArtSci) from the open-source Urban- 362
Scene3D dataset[18]. MatrixCity is sampled from the SmallCity 363
dataset, whereas Hospital and P1 HuiTian are real-world datasets 364
collected from actual urban environments. 365

For benchmarking, EGGS is compared against four SOTA 366
large-scale reconstruction methods: OctreeGS, CityGSv2, Gren- 367
delGS, and ScaffoldGS. All experiments for EGGS, ScaffoldGS, 368
and GrendelGS are conducted on a single Nvidia V100 GPU, with 369
30,000 training iterations. Due to its two-stage design, CityGSv2 370
is trained using 15,000 coarse iterations followed by 15,000 fine- 371
tuning iterations. 372

For quantitative evaluation, we adopt PSNR, SSIM[43], and 373
LPIPS[48] to comprehensively assess reconstruction performance. 374
Additionally, to highlight EGGS’s effectiveness in addressing 375
viewpoint-local coverage imbalance and redundant computa- 376
tion, we further report the standard deviation (Std) of PSNR 377
across viewpoints as a measure of reconstruction consistency, and 378
the total training time (min) over 30,000 iterations as an indicator 379
of computational efficiency. For a fair comparison, all models are 380
trained with identical hyperparameter settings, which are detailed 381
in the supplementary material. 382

4.2. Rendering Quality 383

EGGS achieves superior rendering performance on both the syn- 384
thetic MatrixCity and real-world datasets through multi-expert 385
guided optimization. As shown in Tab. 1 and Fig. 4, its overall 386
reconstruction quality matches or surpasses current SOTA meth- 387
ods, with consistently higher PSNR values up to a 5% gain on the 388
Hospital dataset. On the Residence dataset, EGGS’s SSIM and 389
LPIPS are slightly lower than the best results. We attribute this 390
to the dataset’s dense residential layout, where a single viewpoint 391
already covers abundant scene content, yielding a natural effect 392
similar to EGGS’s experts. This observation highlights the im- 393
portance of viewpoint coverage in large-scale reconstruction and 394
explains the close performance between EGGS and other SOTA 395
baselines in this case. For the MatrixCity scene, the relatively low 396
performance of OctreeGS may partially stem from conflicts caused 397
by using unified hyperparameter settings across all methods. 398

4.4. Hyperparameter Analysis and Ablation 451

In EGGS, the hyperparameter top_n controls the expert model’s 452
scale. A lower value limits coverage of Gaussian anchors need- 453
ing guidance, while a higher value causes redundancy and greater 454
overhead. Fig. 7 shows optimal rendering occurs when top_n is 455
between 0.15 and 0.2. 456

To evaluate the role of each module in the EGGS algorithm, 457
we conducted ablation experiments, and the results are shown in 458
Tab. 3. We first investigate whether the semantic segmentation 459
prior has a significant influence on the final reconstruction qual- 460
ity. To this end, we remove the semantic segmentation module 461
and replace it with a strategy that selects experts based on the in- 462
formation richness between images and point clouds. As shown 463
in the results, the difference is minor. We attribute this to the fact 464
that semantic segmentation serves as a suggestive classification 465
mechanism for category expert selection, while the final expert 466
guidance relies on feature similarity and the multi-scale stable 467
matching algorithm, which dynamically reassign semantic labels 468
based on high-similarity features. Therefore, possible misclassifi- 469
cations in semantic segmentation have limited impact on the final 470
reconstruction, as the matching process corrects such errors during 471
optimization. 472
