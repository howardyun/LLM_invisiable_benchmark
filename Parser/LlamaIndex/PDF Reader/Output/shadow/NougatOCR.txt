### Overview

EGGS is a large-scale collaborative reconstruction framework based on a semantic partitioned multi-expert model. Instead of dividing scenes into geographic blocks, EGGS establishes a complete reconstruction pipeline through two major systems: an expert-guided framework and expert collaborative reconstruction. These correspond to three key modules: Semantic-Prior Filtering, Expert Division & Guidance and Expert Collaborative Reconstruction.

The semantic prior filtering module employs a dual filtering mechanism based on semantics and clustering to select viewpoints and corresponding point clouds that are semantically rich and expert-specific reconstruction regions, while viewpoint clustering. These selected viewpoints consistently within each expert model.

The expert-guided reconstruction module treats these experts as reconstruction priors. During the preliminary reconstruction stage, EGGS performs stable matching between experts and spatially scattered yet semantically consistent elements across the scene, enabling one-to-one expert guidance. Through a graph neuroscience work, prior knowledge is propagated from experts to guide the reconstruction of these elements, achieving high-quality and balanced results. This design effectively mitigates the reconstruction quality disparity and redundant computation "rediscovered" and "relearned") caused by viewpoint locality and isolated rendering of repeated structures.

Finally, the expert collaboration mechanism addresses the intrinsic competition-cooperation problem introduced by replacing geographic partitioning with a multi-expert framework. By assigning gradient weights based on rendering quality, EGGS promotes expert cooperation during reconstruction. Additionally, an opacity-based inter-expert constraint reduces optimization conflicts at expert boundaries, alleviating edge blurring and ensuring coherent global reconstruction.

### Expert-Guided Framework

#### Semantic-Prior Filtering

**Semantic-prior filtering module** of EGGS takes camera viewpoints, point clouds, and semantic segmentation masks as input to generate a subset of viewpoints and point clouds prioritized for expert reconstruction. This module incorporates a Segmented-Prior filtering mechanism consisting of Segmented Filter and viewpoint clustering. The semantic segmentation mask prior is obtained using the CLIP+SAM[15, 29] model guided by textual prompts to capture semantic regions, as detailed in the supplementary material, including model parameters and settings. Semantic score filtering selects the viewpoints and point clouds that best represent expert-specific reconstruction regions, while viewpoint clustering ensures viewpoints consistency within each expert model.

**Step 1: Segmented Filter.** To quantify the scene information richness of all semantic categories within each viewpoint and prioritize viewpoints that capture more structural details for high-quality expert reconstruction, we define the semantic score as a measure of the semantic richness captured by a single viewpoint. This score is computed through the interaction between the 3D point cloud constructed via SfM[33] and the semantic masks associated with each viewpoint.

Each 3D point has a unique index $pid_{j}$ and associated 2D keypoint coordinates $\text{sys}\in\mathbb{R}^{N_{i}\times 2}$, where $N_{i}$ denotes the number of viewpoints in which the point is visible. These coordinates represent the projection positions and visibility of each 3D point across viewpoints. For a given viewpoint $cam_{i}$, its semantic score $s_{cam_{i}}$ is obtained by intersecting all semantic masks of that viewpoint with its corresponding keypoint coordinates and summing over the projected counts:

$$s_{cam_{i}}=\sum_{k\in K}\sum_{j}pid_{j}\;\;when\;\;\;\text{$\mathit{xys}_{j} \in M_{k}$}. \tag{1}$$

Figure 2: EGGS includes a Semantic-Prior Filtering module for selecting expert models from semantically rich viewpoints and an Expert-Guided module that enhances overall reconstruction via stable matching and GNN.

Here, $K$ denotes the set of all semantic categories, $k\in K$ represents the $k$-th semantic subcategory, and $M_{k}$ is the semantic mask of category $k$ in viewpoint $cam_{i}$. A higher semantic score $s_{cam_{i}}$ indicates that the viewpoint contains richer semantic information across all categories, making it more favorable for high-quality reconstruction. Therefore, $s_{cam_{i}}$, serves as a crucial criterion for selecting viewpoints assigned to expert models.

**Step 2: Viewpoint Clustering.** Relying solely on semantic richness to select viewpoints and point clouds for expert reconstruction may compromise viewpoints consistency, leading to see-22 were viewpoint locality issues. To address this, we introduce an additional _viewpoint clustering_ stage based on the K-Means algorithm, which partitions all viewpoints into multiple clusters according to their camera parameters.

Within each cluster, viewpoints are ranked by their semantic scores $s_{cam_{i}}$. A threshold hyperparameter $Top_{n}\%$ is then applied to select the top $n\%$ viewpoints and their corresponding point clouds for expert-prior reconstruction. This process ensures that each expert receives semantically rich yet spatially diverse in-231 puts, thereby preserving viewpoints consistency and improving reconstruction balance across large-scale scenes.

#### 3.2.2 Expert Division & Guidance.

**Expert Construction.** The reconstruction isolation and redundant 235 computation issues caused by viewpoint locality essentially stem from the absence of a unified global paradigm to guide and constrain reconstruction across spatially distributed objects. Under limited viewpoint coverage, identical structures cannot be consistently captured from a global perspective, resulting in fragmented 240 reconstructions.

To address this, EGGS constructs multiple category-specific experts using the semantically rich viewpoints and corresponding point clouds obtained from the semantic prior filtering module. These selected data are first reconstructed through a priority reconstruction process, generating high-quality Gaussian anchors that serve as the foundation of each expert. Each category expert is composed of Gaussian anchors corresponding to its semantic category and acts as a global prior to guide consistent reconstruction across the entire scene, as illustrated in Fig. 2.

**Stage 1: Multi-Scale Stable Matching.** The stable matching algorithm connects the multi-category experts obtained from priority reconstruction with their corresponding semantic instances across the scene, regardless of geographic separation. By establishing associations purely based on feature similarity rather than spatial proximity, this process eliminates reconstruction isolation caused by inconsistent reconstruction of semantically identical objects distributed across large-scale scenes.

Considering that the classical stable matching algorithm has a computational complexity of $O(n^{2})$, performing global feature association among all Gaussian anchors is computationally prohibitive. To address this, we propose a multi-scale stable matching scheme based on an Octree hierarchical structure, which reduces the overall time complexity from $O(n^{2})$ to $O(n\log n)$, thus meeting the efficiency requirements of expert-guided reconstruction in large-scale scenarios, as shown in Fig. 3.

Specifically, for each semantic class, the algorithm iteratively performs stable matching within Octree sub-blocks, pairing Gaussian anchors from category-specific experts with those of scene instances exhibiting similar feature representations obtained from preliminary reconstruction, as illustrated in Fig. 2. This matching process can be formulated using a bijective function $\mu_{\text{mupot}}$, ensuring that each Gaussian anchor $a\in unopt$ from the scene-object Gaussian anchor set (unopt) is paired with a corresponding Gaussian anchor $b\in opt$ from the expert (opt) of the same semantic category:

$$\mu_{\text{unopt}}(a)=\arg\max_{b\in S_{opt}}s_{unopt}(b), \tag{2}$$

subject to the following constraint:

$$s_{opt}(a)>s_{opt}(\mu_{\text{unopt}}(a))\cap s_{unopt}(b)>s_{unopt}(\mu_{ \text{unopt}}^{-1}(a)), \tag{3}$$

where $s_{opt}$ and $s_{unopt}$ denote the similarity preference matrices for expert Gaussian anchors and scene Gaussian anchors of the same category, respectively. The similarity is measured based on the spatial feature embeddings captured by each Gaussian anchor. Since the distributions of $opt$ and $unopt$ anchors within Octree sub-blocks are not always uniform, the hierarchical multi-level matching ensures that any unmatched anchors in higher levels can still be matched in lower levels or during global matching, thereby maintaining completeness and stability of the expert guidance pro

**Stage 2: Graph Neural Network Optimization.** EGGS equips each targeted expert model with a lightweight graph neural network[32, 36, 40] for parameter learning and guidance. Given a Graph Neural Network with node set $V_{gnn}=V_{o}\cup V_{u}$, where $V_{o}$ is the set of opt and $V_{i}$ is the set of output, for nodes $a\in V_{u}$ and $b\in V_{o}$, the edge set $E$ is defined as:

$$E_{gnn}=\{(b,a)\in V_{o}\times V_{u}\mid\exists M\subseteq V_{o}\times V_{u} \text{ s.t. }(b,a)\in M\}, \tag{4}$$

where $S_{ba}:V_{o}\times V_{u}\rightarrow\mathbb{R}$ is the similarity preference function between nodes $b\in V_{o}$ and $a\in V_{u}$, and $M$ is a stable matching relation. Corresponding to the sparse graph relationships constructed for stable matching, a two-layer GAT network is employed, with each layer consisting of four attention heads. Further information and code will be presented in the supplementary material.

In graph neural networks with node set $V_{gnn}$ and edge set $E_{gnn}$, the optimization of offsets, scales, and rotation matrices expert parameters can guide global scene gaussian anchors which are initialized throught preliminary reconstruction to approximate object geometry rapidly and adapt further to scene-specific variation. The losses for the three parameters are defined as $L_{offsets}$, $L_{scaling}$, and $L_{rotation}$. Specifically, $L_{offsets}$ is computed using Euclidean distance, $L_{scaling}$ is measured via mean squared error (MSE), and $L_{rotation}$ is based on cosine distance between expert and non-expert representations. The overall loss of the graph neural network during expert model optimization is formulated as:

$$L_{g}=L_{offsets}+L_{scaling}+L_{rotation}. \tag{5}$$

### Expert Collaborative Reconstruction

To address boundary ambiguity and collaboration challenges in multi-expert models, EGGS introduces two mechanisms that convert Gaussian anchors with ambiguous attribution into collaboratingv birds across expert domains.

**Gradient Contribution Allocation**. All expert models share a total loss function, but in each iteration, the loss is distributed according to each expert's rendering contribution weight to enable coordinated optimization. Suppose there are experts corresponding to $K$ semantic categories, with the total loss function denoted as $L$. The contribution of expert $k$ in each rendering is $\alpha_{k}$, represented by Gaussian anchor weights, satisfying $\sum_{k=1}^{K}\alpha_{k}=1$. Each expert's parameters are $\theta_{k}$, the effective update loss is $L_{\text{exp}}$, and the gradient of the total loss with respect to the parameters is $\nabla_{\theta_{k}}L$. The gradient assigned to each expert is $\nabla_{\theta_{k}}L_{\text{exp}}$:

$$\nabla_{\theta_{k}}L_{\text{exp}}=\alpha_{k}\nabla_{\theta_{k}}L. \tag{6}$$

**Boundary Gaussian anchors Collaboration**. For Gaussian anchors at the boundaries of expert model view frustums, where multiple experts may render and optimize these anchors differently, EGGS adopts a gravitational model from classical mechanics to quantify the influence of each expert's boundary Gaussian anchors on others within the region, facilitating multi-expert collaboration. EGGS uses the Mahalanobis distance $d_{Math}(i,j)$ to consider the shape and rotation of Gaussian anchors, defining $\mu_{j}-\mu_{i}$ as the direction vector:

$$d_{Math}(i,j)=\sqrt{(\mu_{j}-\mu_{i})^{T}\sum_{\bar{\imath}}^{-1}(\mu_{j}-\mu_{ i})}, \tag{7}$$

which yields the formula for the influence $F_{i\to j}$ of an expert's boundary Gaussian anchors on co-located Gaussian anchors of other experts:

$$F_{i\to j}=\frac{\alpha_{i}\cdot\alpha_{j}}{d_{Math}(i,j)^{2}}\cdot(\mu_{j}- \mu_{i}), \tag{8}$$

where $\alpha_{i}$ and $\alpha_{j}$ represent the opacity of two Gaussian anchors, here extended to denote their mass weights, and $x_{i}$ and $x_{j}$ are the center positions of these Gaussian anchors.

### Experimental Setup

**Dataset Configuration**. EGGS is evaluated on five large-scale datasets, including one synthetic urban dataset (MatrixCity[16]), two real-world datasets (Hospital and P1 HuiTian), and two complex scenes (Residence and ArtSci) from the open-source Urban-Scene3D dataset[18]. MatrixCity is sampled from the SmallCity dataset, whereas Hospital and P1 HuiTian are real-world datasets collected from actual urban environments.

For benchmarking, EGGS is compared against four SOTA large-scale reconstruction methods: _OctreeG5_, _CityG5v2_, _GrendeGS_, and _ScaffoldGS_. All experiments for EGGS, ScaffoldGS, and GrendeGS are conducted on a single Nvidia 100 GPU, with 30,000 training iterations. Due to its two-stage design, _CityG5v2_ is trained using 15,000 coarse iterations followed by 15,000 fine-tuning iterations.

For quantitative evaluation, we adopt PSNR, SSIM[43], and LPIPS[48] to comprehensively assess reconstruction performance. Additionally, to highlight EGGS's effectiveness in addressing **viewpoint-local coverage imbalance** and **redundant computation**, we further report the standard deviation (**Sdt**) of PSNR across viewpoints as a measure of reconstruction consistency, and the total training time (**min**) over 30,000 iterations as an indicator of computational efficiency. For a fair comparison, all models are trained with identical hyperparameter settings, which are detailed in the supplementary material.

### Rendering Quality

EGGS achieves superior rendering performance on both the synthetic MatrixCity and real-world datasets through multi-expert guided optimization. As shown in Tab. 1 and Fig. 4, its overall reconstruction quality matches or surpasses current SOTA methods, with consistently higher PSNR values up to a **5%** gain on the Hospital dataset. On the Residence dataset, EGGS's SSIM and LPIPS are slightly lower than the best results. We attribute this to the dataset's dense residential layout, where a single viewpoint already covers abundant scene content, yielding a natural effect similar to EGGS's experts. This observation highlights the importance of viewpoint coverage in large-scale reconstruction and explains the close performance between EGGS and other SOTA baselines in this case. For the MatrixCity scene, the relatively low performance of OctreeGS may partially stem from conflicts caused by using unified hyperparameter settings across all methods.

### Hyperparameter Analysis and Ablation

In EGGS, the hyperparameter _top_$n$_ controls the expert model's scale. A lower value limits coverage of Gaussian anchors needing guidance, while a higher value causes redundancy and greater overhead. Fig. 7 shows optimal rendering occurs when _top_$n$_ is between 0.15 and 0.2.

To evaluate the role of each module in the EGGS algorithm, we conducted ablation experiments, and the results are shown in Tab. 3. We first investigate whether the **semantic segmentation prior** has a significant influence on the final reconstruction quality. To this end, we remove the semantic segmentation module and replace it with a strategy that selects experts based on the **information richness** between images and point clouds. As shown in the results, the difference is minor. We attribute this to the fact that semantic segmentation serves as a **suggestive classification mechanism** for category expert selection, while the final expert guidance relies on **feature similarity** and the **multi-scale stable matching algorithm**, which dynamically reassign semantic labels based on high-similarity features. Therefore, possible misclassifications in semantic segmentation have limited impact on the final reconstruction, as the matching process corrects such errors during optimization.

### Future and Limitation

EGGS demonstrates outstanding performance in addressing the two major challenges posed by the local coverage of large-scale scene perspectives. Currently, the evolving 3D spatial semantic segmentation technology[51] seems to be able to replace the original 2D semantic prior, which had limitations in accuracy, providing EGGS with a more outstanding prior enhancement.

## 5 Conclusion

This paper presents EGGS, an expert-guided framework for large-scale scene reconstruction. EGGS identifies the viewpoint locality in large-scale scenes, which gives rise to two challenges: reconstruction isolation and redundant computation. By leveraging semantic priors to construct experts and employing an expert-guided reconstruction strategy, EGGS mitigates isolation-induced quality inconsistency and achieves competitive reconstruction efficiency. An expert collaboration mechanism further resolves the boundary ambiguity introduced by experts. Experiments show that existing methods degrade under viewpoint locality, whereas EGGS maintains balanced cross-view reconstruction quality and achieves SOTA performance across diverse datasets, with improvements of up to 5% in PSNR.
