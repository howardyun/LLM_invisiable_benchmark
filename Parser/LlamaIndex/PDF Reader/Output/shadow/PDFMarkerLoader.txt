## 1. Introduction 023

NeRF [1, 25] and 3DGS [9, 13] have demonstrated efficient 3D 024 reconstruction capabilities across diverse scales, ranging from 025 small indoor scenes [22] to large outdoor environments, sup- 026 porting downstream tasks such as SLAM and AR content cre- 027 ation [37]. Current large-scale reconstruction methods [17, 21, 028 38, 42, 45] typically adopt geographic region-based parallel algo- 029 rithms, where each subregion is independently optimized to accel- 030 erate reconstruction. However, these methods fail to address the 031 viewpoint locality problem inherent in large-scale scenes. Unlike 032 indoor settings with near-complete scene capture, each viewpoint 033 034 in outdoor scenes only observes a partial area, leading to three ma035 jor issues. 036 First, **Reconstruction Isolation**. Each subregion reconstruc037 tion relies on the viewpoints that observe its internal content. 038 Since viewpoints only provide limited coverage within individ039 ual regions, reconstruction quality varies significantly across sub040 regions, an issue unique to large-scale reconstruction. Recent 041 work [20] attempts to improve inter-region consistency via addi042 tional loss constraints, yet viewpoint locality remains unresolved:
043 high-quality subregions fail to enhance adjacent low-quality ones, 044 ultimately degrading overall reconstruction fidelity. 045 Second, **Redundant Computation**. Geographic partition046 ing separates semantic entities such as buildings, vegetation, and 047 roads; due to viewpoint locality, these elements are repeatedly 048 "rediscovered" and "relearned". This scheme overlooks seman049 tic and structural redundancy, causing repetitive subregion recon050 structions that raise computational costs and reduce overall recon051 struction quality. 052 Third, **Multi-Expert Collaboration and Competition**. 053 Modular-GS [34] introduce MoEs [31] into 3DGS for semantic 054 space decomposition. Inspired by the expert division strategy in 055 MOEs for 3D reconstruction, we propose that an expert-guided 056 structure can go beyond semantic segmentation and serve as an 057 alternative to geographic partitioning. However, the multi-experts 058 structure naturally involves expert competition and cooperation, 059 which necessitates designing collaborative mechanisms to prevent 060 boundary ambiguity that could impair rendering quality. 061 To address these challenges, we present **EGGS**, an Expert062 Guided Framework Constructed from Semantic Priors for Large063 Scale Scene Reconstruction, which replaces geographic partition064 ing with a semantic prior multi-expert guided framework. EGGS 065 significantly improves reconstruction balance across large-scale 066 scenes and reducing computational cost for higher-quality recon067 struction across the whole scene. At its core, EGGS leverages 068 an expert-guided strategy to balance and accelerate scene recon069 struction through three key parts: First, semantic segmentation di070 vides the scene into multiple categories, each assigned to a cat071 egory expert trained with semantically rich viewpoints for corre072 sponding instance learning and reconstruction. Third, a collab076 orative and constraint-based mechanism promotes expert cooper077 ation and suppresses inter-expert conflicts, alleviating boundary078 induced blurring. 079 Extensive experiments in Fig. 6 and Tab. 2 demonstrates that 080 EGGS achieves more balanced and efficient reconstruction than 081 spatial partitioning methods, improving overall reconstruction 082 quality by up to 5% in PSNR. ## 2. Related Work 101 2.1. Large-Scale Scene Reconstruction 102

The conflict between high reconstruction costs and low accuracy 103 in large-scale scene reconstruction, driven by scene complexity, re- 104 mains a key challenge in 3D reconstruction. Recent NeRF variants 105 using heuristic partitioning [38, 39, 44] or voxel grids [2, 6, 26, 45] 106 have supported performance in large-scale scenes but are lim- 107 ited by long training times. Alternatively, 3D Gaussian Splatting 108 (3DGS) has introduced methods like progressive [14, 17, 49] and 109 level-of-detail (LOD) schemes [21, 30] to balance quality and ef- 110 ficiency. However, these geographically partitioned methods still 111 suffer from inconsistent reconstruction quality due to viewpoint 112 limitations, leading to degraded overall results. 113

## 2.2. Semantic-Awareness 114

With advancements in 2D semantic understanding [15, 29], recent 115 research has explored semantic integration in 3D reconstruction 116 [3, 8, 28, 47]. Some methods embed semantic information into 117 explicit 3D Gaussian representations by projecting 2D semantic 118 features or aggregating semantic pools [7]. Others leverage ex- 119 ternal semantic models to guide 3DGS training or inference [51], 120 such as introducing semantic viewpoints consistency losses to mit- 121 igate overfitting [11, 41]. These efforts highlight the potential of 122 semantic-aware methods in 3D reconstruction [27], yet their appli- 123 cation in complex, large-scale scenes requires further exploration. 124 Inspired by the growing field of 3D spatial semantic awareness, 125 we aim to leverage rich semantic information in large-scale scenes 126 to overcome viewpoint limitations and enable optimization regard- 127 less of spatial distance. 128

## 2.3. Moes And Multi-Expert Framework 129

MoEs framework [10, 12, 35]enables parallel training through ex- 130 pert specialization. Recent successes in visual imaging [31, 46] 131 have inspired its application in 3D reconstruction. Existing work 132 has combined MoEs with 3D reconstruction [4, 5, 19, 34] and 133 made improvements for large-scale scenes [24, 50]. However, 134 prior works have largely ignored the role of experts as reusable 135 semantic priors capable of guiding similar objects that frequently 136 appear in large-scale scenes. 137

## 233 **3.2.2. Expert Division & Guidance.**

234 **Expert Construction.** The reconstruction isolation and redundant 235 computation issues caused by viewpoint locality essentially stem 236 from the absence of a unified global paradigm to guide and con237 strain reconstruction across spatially distributed objects. Under 238 limited viewpoint coverage, identical structures cannot be consis239 tently captured from a global perspective, resulting in fragmented 240 reconstructions. 241 To address this, EGGS constructs multiple category-specific 242 experts using the semantically rich viewpoints and correspond243 ing point clouds obtained from the semantic prior filtering mod244 ule. These selected data are first reconstructed through a priority 245 reconstruction process, generating high-quality Gaussian anchors 246 that serve as the foundation of each expert. Each category expert is 247 composed of Gaussian anchors corresponding to its semantic cat248 egory and acts as a global prior to guide consistent reconstruction 249 across the entire scene, as illustrated in Fig. 2. 250 **Stage 1: Multi-Scale Stable Matching.** The stable matching 251 algorithm connects the multi-category experts obtained from pri252 ority reconstruction with their corresponding semantic instances 253 across the scene, regardless of geographic separation. ## 4.4. Hyperparameter Analysis And Ablation 451

In EGGS, the hyperparameter top_n controls the expert model's 452 scale. A lower value limits coverage of Gaussian anchors need- 453 ing guidance, while a higher value causes redundancy and greater 454 overhead. Fig. 7 shows optimal rendering occurs when top_n is 455 between 0.15 and 0.2. 456 To evaluate the role of each module in the EGGS algorithm, 457 we conducted ablation experiments, and the results are shown in 458 Tab. 3. We first investigate whether the semantic segmentation 459 prior has a significant influence on the final reconstruction qual- 460 ity. To this end, we remove the semantic segmentation module 461 and replace it with a strategy that selects experts based on the in- 462 formation richness between images and point clouds. As shown 463 in the results, the difference is minor. We attribute this to the fact 464 that semantic segmentation serves as a suggestive classification 465 mechanism for category expert selection, while the final expert 466 guidance relies on **feature similarity** and the multi-scale stable 467 matching algorithm, which dynamically reassign semantic labels 468 based on high-similarity features. Therefore, possible misclassifi- 469 cations in semantic segmentation have limited impact on the final 470 reconstruction, as the matching process corrects such errors during 471 optimization. 472

## 4.5. Future And Limitation 485

EGGS demonstrates outstanding performance in addressing the 486 two major challenges posed by the local coverage of large-scale 487 scene perspectives. Currently, the evolving 3D spatial semantic 488 segmentation technology[51] seems to be able to replace the orig- 489 inal 2D semantic prior, which had limitations in accuracy, provid- 490 ing EGGS with a more outstanding prior enhancement. 491

## 5. Conclusion 492

This paper presents EGGS, an expert-guided framework for large- 493 scale scene reconstruction. EGGS identifies the viewpoint locality 494 in large-scale scenes, which gives rise to two challenges: recon- 495 struction isolation and redundant computation. By leveraging se- 496 mantic priors to construct experts and employing an expert-guided 497 reconstruction strategy, EGGS mitigates isolation-induced quality 498 inconsistency and achieves competitive reconstruction efficiency. 499 An expert collaboration mechanism further resolves the bound- 500 ary ambiguity introduced by experts. Experiments show that ex- 501 isting methods degrade under viewpoint locality, whereas EGGS 502 maintains balanced cross-view reconstruction quality and achieves 503 SOTA performance across diverse datasets, with improvements of 504 up to 5% in PSNR. 505
