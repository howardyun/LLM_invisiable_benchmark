Neural Radiance Fields (NeRF) and 3D Gaussian Splatting
(3DGS) have demonstrated strong capability in reconstructing 3D
scenes ranging from indoor to outdoor environments. For large-
scale scenes, reconstruction is often accelerated through paral-
lelization based on geographic partitioning. However, excessive
scene size introduces viewpoint locality, leading to two major chal-
lenges: reconstruction isolation and redundant computation. The
former causes significant inconsistency in rendering quality across
viewpoints and the latter leads to repetitive optimization of similar
elements, resulting in wasted computational resources. Inspired
by the expert optimization principle in Mixture-of-Experts (MoEs),
we propose EGGS (Expert-Guided Framework Constructed from
Semantic Priors for Large-Scale Scene Reconstruction), an expert-
guided framework that replaces geographic partitioning with ex-
pert collaboration constructed from semantic priors. EGGS as-
signs experts to scene elements based on semantic and feature
similarity, enabling semantic-aware collaboration that effectively

alleviates isolation and redundancy. Extensive experiments on
both synthetic and real-world datasets demonstrate that EGGS
achieves state-of-the-art reconstruction quality, outperforming ex-
isting methods by up to 5% in PSNR. Code and datasets will be
released upon acceptance.

small indoor scenes [22] to large outdoor environments, sup-
porting downstream tasks such as SLAM and AR content cre-
ation [37]. Current large-scale reconstruction methods [17, 21,
38, 42, 45] typically adopt geographic region-based parallel algo-
rithms, where each subregion is independently optimized to accel-
erate reconstruction. However, these methods fail to address the
viewpoint locality problem inherent in large-scale scenes. Unlike
indoor settings with near-complete scene capture, each viewpoint

First, Reconstruction Isolation. Each subregion reconstruc-
tion relies on the viewpoints that observe its internal content.
Since viewpoints only provide limited coverage within individ-
ual regions, reconstruction quality varies significantly across sub-
regions, an issue unique to large-scale reconstruction. Recent
work [20] attempts to improve inter-region consistency via addi-
tional loss constraints, yet viewpoint locality remains unresolved:
high-quality subregions fail to enhance adjacent low-quality ones,
ultimately degrading overall reconstruction fidelity.

Second, Redundant Computation. Geographic partition-
ing separates semantic entities such as buildings, vegetation, and
roads; due to viewpoint locality, these elements are repeatedly
“rediscovered” and “relearned”. This scheme overlooks seman-
tic and structural redundancy, causing repetitive subregion recon-
structions that raise computational costs and reduce overall recon-
struction quality.

Third, Multi-Expert Collaboration and Competition.
Modular-GS [34] introduce MoEs [31] into 3DGS for semantic
space decomposition. Inspired by the expert division strategy in
MOEs for 3D reconstruction, we propose that an expert-guided
structure can go beyond semantic segmentation and serve as an
alternative to geographic partitioning. However, the multi-experts
structure naturally involves expert competition and cooperation,
which necessitates designing collaborative mechanisms to prevent
boundary ambiguity that could impair rendering quality.

To address these challenges, we present EGGS, an Expert-
Guided Framework Constructed from Semantic Priors for Large-
Scale Scene Reconstruction, which replaces geographic partition-
ing with a semantic prior multi-expert guided framework. EGGS
significantly improves reconstruction balance across large-scale
scenes and reducing computational cost for higher-quality recon-
struction across the whole scene. At its core, EGGS leverages
an expert-guided strategy to balance and accelerate scene recon-
struction through three key parts: First, semantic segmentation di-
vides the scene into multiple categories, each assigned to a cat-
egory expert trained with semantically rich viewpoints for corre-
sponding instance learning and reconstruction. Second, category
experts further guide the reconstruction of unselected viewpoints,
mitigating reconstruction isolation caused by viewpoint locality
and achieving balanced, efficient reconstruction. Third, a collab-
orative and constraint-based mechanism promotes expert cooper-
ation and suppresses inter-expert conflicts, alleviating boundary-
induced blurring.

Extensive experiments in Fig. 6 and Tab. 2 demonstrates that
EGGS achieves more balanced and efficient reconstruction than
spatial partitioning methods,
improving overall reconstruction
quality by up to 5% in PSNR. As presented in Fig. 1, EGGS
provides more accurate and detailed reconstructions than existing
large-scale approaches. Our main contributions are summarized
as follows:
1. We propose a novel expert-guided framework for large-scale
3D reconstruction, addressing rendering isolation and redun-
dant computation inherent in geographic partitioning methods.
To our best knowledge, this is the first work to apply expert
guidance for large-scale reconstruction.

2. We design a complete expert construction and training pipeline

that effectively improves reconstruction balance while reducing
overall computational cost.

3. We introduce a contribution-weighted expert collaboration al-
gorithm that mitigates boundary ambiguity caused by inter-
expert competition.

4. Extensive experiments on both synthetic and real-world
datasets validate that EGGS improving overall reconstruction
quality by up to 5% in PSNR while improving balance and ef-
ficiency.

2. Related Work

2.1. Large-scale Scene Reconstruction

The conflict between high reconstruction costs and low accuracy
in large-scale scene reconstruction, driven by scene complexity, re-
mains a key challenge in 3D reconstruction. Recent NeRF variants
using heuristic partitioning [38, 39, 44] or voxel grids [2, 6, 26, 45]
have supported performance in large-scale scenes but are lim-
ited by long training times. Alternatively, 3D Gaussian Splatting
(3DGS) has introduced methods like progressive [14, 17, 49] and
level-of-detail (LOD) schemes [21, 30] to balance quality and ef-
ficiency. However, these geographically partitioned methods still
suffer from inconsistent reconstruction quality due to viewpoint
limitations, leading to degraded overall results.

2.2. Semantic-awareness

With advancements in 2D semantic understanding [15, 29], recent
research has explored semantic integration in 3D reconstruction
[3, 8, 28, 47]. Some methods embed semantic information into
explicit 3D Gaussian representations by projecting 2D semantic
features or aggregating semantic pools [7]. Others leverage ex-
ternal semantic models to guide 3DGS training or inference [51],
such as introducing semantic viewpoints consistency losses to mit-
igate overfitting [11, 41]. These efforts highlight the potential of
semantic-aware methods in 3D reconstruction [27], yet their appli-
cation in complex, large-scale scenes requires further exploration.
Inspired by the growing field of 3D spatial semantic awareness,
we aim to leverage rich semantic information in large-scale scenes
to overcome viewpoint limitations and enable optimization regard-
less of spatial distance.

2.3. MoEs and Multi-Expert Framework

MoEs framework [10, 12, 35]enables parallel training through ex-
pert specialization. Recent successes in visual imaging [31, 46]
have inspired its application in 3D reconstruction. Existing work
has combined MoEs with 3D reconstruction [4, 5, 19, 34] and
made improvements for large-scale scenes [24, 50]. However,
prior works have largely ignored the role of experts as reusable
semantic priors capable of guiding similar objects that frequently
appear in large-scale scenes.

The semantic prior filtering module employs a dual filtering

balanced results. This design effectively mitigates the reconstruc-

Semantic-prior filtering module of EGGS takes camera view-
points, point clouds, and semantic segmentation masks as input to
generate a subset of viewpoints and point clouds prioritized for ex-
pert reconstruction. This module incorporates a Segmented-Prior

clustering. The semantic segmentation mask prior is obtained us-

capture semantic regions, as detailed in the supplementary mate-

expert-specific reconstruction regions, while viewpoint clustering
ensures viewpoints consistency within each expert model.

Step 2: Viewpoint Clustering. Relying solely on semantic
richness to select viewpoints and point clouds for expert recon-
struction may compromise viewpoints consistency, leading to se-
vere viewpoint locality issues. To address this, we introduce an
additional viewpoint clustering stage based on the K-Means al-
gorithm, which partitions all viewpoints into multiple clusters ac-
cording to their camera parameters.

Within each cluster, viewpoints are ranked by their semantic
scores scami . A threshold hyperparameter T op_n% is then ap-
plied to select the top n% viewpoints and their corresponding
point clouds for expert-prior reconstruction. This process ensures
that each expert receives semantically rich yet spatially diverse in-
puts, thereby preserving viewpoints consistency and improving re-
construction balance across large-scale scenes.

3.2.2. Expert Division & Guidance.

Expert Construction. The reconstruction isolation and redundant
computation issues caused by viewpoint locality essentially stem
from the absence of a unified global paradigm to guide and con-
strain reconstruction across spatially distributed objects. Under
limited viewpoint coverage, identical structures cannot be consis-
tently captured from a global perspective, resulting in fragmented
reconstructions.

To address this, EGGS constructs multiple category-specific
experts using the semantically rich viewpoints and correspond-
ing point clouds obtained from the semantic prior filtering mod-
ule. These selected data are first reconstructed through a priority
reconstruction process, generating high-quality Gaussian anchors
that serve as the foundation of each expert. Each category expert is
composed of Gaussian anchors corresponding to its semantic cat-
egory and acts as a global prior to guide consistent reconstruction
across the entire scene, as illustrated in Fig. 2.

Stage 1: Multi-Scale Stable Matching. The stable matching
algorithm connects the multi-category experts obtained from pri-
ority reconstruction with their corresponding semantic instances
across the scene, regardless of geographic separation. By estab-
lishing associations purely based on feature similarity rather than
spatial proximity, this process eliminates reconstruction isolation
caused by inconsistent reconstruction of semantically identical ob-
jects distributed across large-scale scenes.

Considering that the classical stable matching algorithm has
a computational complexity of O(n2), performing global feature
association among all Gaussian anchors is computationally pro-
hibitive. To address this, we propose a multi-scale stable matching
scheme based on an Octree hierarchical structure, which reduces
the overall time complexity from O(n2) to O(n log n), thus meet-
ing the efficiency requirements of expert-guided reconstruction in
large-scale scenarios, as shown in Fig. 3.

Specifically, for each semantic class, the algorithm iteratively
performs stable matching within Octree sub-blocks, pairing Gaus-

4. Experiment

This section presents the experimental evaluation of EGGS. Sec-
tion 4.1 introduces the datasets, baseline algorithms and experi-
mental setup. Section 4.2 compares EGGS with existing methods

in terms of rendering quality across five datasets. Section 4.3 em-
ploys radar charts to demonstrate EGGS’s improvements in bal-
ancing per-view rendering quality and alleviating isolation caused
by perspective locality. Section 4.4 explores the impact of key hy-
perparameters and presents ablation studies to evaluate the contri-
bution of each component. Finally, Section 4.5 discusses potential
improvements and outlines directions for future work.

4.1. Experimental Setup

Dataset Configuration. EGGS is evaluated on five large-scale
datasets, including one synthetic urban dataset (MatrixCity[16]),
two real-world datasets (Hospital and P1 HuiTian), and two com-
plex scenes (Residence and ArtSci) from the open-source Urban-
Scene3D dataset[18]. MatrixCity is sampled from the SmallCity
dataset, whereas Hospital and P1 HuiTian are real-world datasets
collected from actual urban environments.

For benchmarking, EGGS is compared against four SOTA
large-scale reconstruction methods: OctreeGS, CityGSv2, Gren-
delGS, and ScaffoldGS. All experiments for EGGS, ScaffoldGS,
and GrendelGS are conducted on a single Nvidia V100 GPU, with
30,000 training iterations. Due to its two-stage design, CityGSv2
is trained using 15,000 coarse iterations followed by 15,000 fine-
tuning iterations.

For quantitative evaluation, we adopt PSNR, SSIM[43], and
LPIPS[48] to comprehensively assess reconstruction performance.
Additionally,
to highlight EGGS’s effectiveness in addressing
viewpoint-local coverage imbalance and redundant computa-
tion, we further report the standard deviation (Std) of PSNR
across viewpoints as a measure of reconstruction consistency, and
the total training time (min) over 30,000 iterations as an indicator
of computational efficiency. For a fair comparison, all models are
trained with identical hyperparameter settings, which are detailed
in the supplementary material.

4.2. Rendering Quality

EGGS achieves superior rendering performance on both the syn-
thetic MatrixCity and real-world datasets through multi-expert
guided optimization. As shown in Tab. 1 and Fig. 4, its overall
reconstruction quality matches or surpasses current SOTA meth-
ods, with consistently higher PSNR values up to a 5% gain on the
Hospital dataset. On the Residence dataset, EGGS’s SSIM and
LPIPS are slightly lower than the best results. We attribute this
to the dataset’s dense residential layout, where a single viewpoint
already covers abundant scene content, yielding a natural effect
similar to EGGS’s experts. This observation highlights the im-
portance of viewpoint coverage in large-scale reconstruction and
explains the close performance between EGGS and other SOTA
baselines in this case. For the MatrixCity scene, the relatively low
performance of OctreeGS may partially stem from conflicts caused
by using unified hyperparameter settings across all methods.

4.4. Hyperparameter Analysis and Ablation

In EGGS, the hyperparameter top_n controls the expert model’s
scale. A lower value limits coverage of Gaussian anchors need-
ing guidance, while a higher value causes redundancy and greater
overhead. Fig. 7 shows optimal rendering occurs when top_n is
between 0.15 and 0.2.

To evaluate the role of each module in the EGGS algorithm,
we conducted ablation experiments, and the results are shown in
Tab. 3. We first investigate whether the semantic segmentation
prior has a significant influence on the final reconstruction qual-
ity. To this end, we remove the semantic segmentation module
and replace it with a strategy that selects experts based on the in-
formation richness between images and point clouds. As shown
in the results, the difference is minor. We attribute this to the fact
that semantic segmentation serves as a suggestive classification
mechanism for category expert selection, while the final expert
guidance relies on feature similarity and the multi-scale stable
matching algorithm, which dynamically reassign semantic labels
based on high-similarity features. Therefore, possible misclassifi-
cations in semantic segmentation have limited impact on the final
reconstruction, as the matching process corrects such errors during
optimization.

4.5. Future and Limitation

EGGS demonstrates outstanding performance in addressing the
two major challenges posed by the local coverage of large-scale
scene perspectives. Currently, the evolving 3D spatial semantic
segmentation technology[51] seems to be able to replace the orig-
inal 2D semantic prior, which had limitations in accuracy, provid-
ing EGGS with a more outstanding prior enhancement.

5. Conclusion

This paper presents EGGS, an expert-guided framework for large-
scale scene reconstruction. EGGS identifies the viewpoint locality
in large-scale scenes, which gives rise to two challenges: recon-
struction isolation and redundant computation. By leveraging se-
mantic priors to construct experts and employing an expert-guided
reconstruction strategy, EGGS mitigates isolation-induced quality
inconsistency and achieves competitive reconstruction efficiency.
An expert collaboration mechanism further resolves the bound-
ary ambiguity introduced by experts. Experiments show that ex-
isting methods degrade under viewpoint locality, whereas EGGS
maintains balanced cross-view reconstruction quality and achieves
SOTA performance across diverse datasets, with improvements of
up to 5% in PSNR.
